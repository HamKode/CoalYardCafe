History Of The Coal Yard Cafe

A cafe built inside a piece of Ithaca history.

The name comes from the building itself. Long before it became a place for breakfast and lunch, this address was part of Ithaca's coal trade and daily life.

Why is it called The Coal Yard Cafe?

Near the original entrance sits a large piece of coal carved with the words Coal Office. For years, this building housed the businesses that sold heating coal to Ithaca residents, and traces of that past are still part of the cafe today.

History you can still see inside

The brass balance beam from the truck scales is still in the dining area, and the old business safe now lives on as the Safe Table inside the cafe.

Coal selling began here in 1901 at 143 Maple Avenue through the Stevens and Cornell East Hill Coal Yard. The Cornell name belonged to Francis C. Cornell, grandson of Ezra Cornell, founder of Cornell University.

From coal yard to cafe

1901 and after

The property became part of Ithaca's coal business, receiving coal shipped from Pennsylvania by rail to the Lehigh Valley Railroad side track in the yard.

Across Maple Avenue

The East Ithaca Railroad Depot once stood nearby, where many Cornell students first arrived in Ithaca.

Until 1960

The coal yard passed through several owners and names. The last was the Perry Coal Company, whose restored sign still hangs on the outer east wall.

Since 2006

Before full cooking was allowed on-site, the cafe served pastries, breads, and lunch treats prepared elsewhere.

Opened in 2013

The Coal Yard Cafe officially opened in the historic Perry Coal Office building, bringing new life to a neighborhood landmark with breakfast, lunch, and a kitchen of its own.
